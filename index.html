About Me

Learning to code at 54 is not normal. Nor is retiring from a career in brain surgery at 54. I am, however, delighted to be in this position.

Having my first career behind me, I am able to pursue my other interests and to realize some of the ideas I have accumulated (some in frustration) during my first career. While in practice not a day would pass that I would not encounter an inefficency in programming or suboptimal interaction with technology that could be remedied with good programming, better interfaces, and improved instruction. This applies to the medical field as well as in day-to-day life.

In retirement I have decided to pursue a path that allows me continued interaction with my old colleagues while I aquire new skills and further myself with programming. I am looking forward to developing programs and systems that smoothen the interactions between health care providers, health care data, and administrative systems. I am certain that my prior experience and new cross-disciplinary skills position me well to identify shortcomings and causes of frustration in current systems and then to design and build new systems for positive change. As I am living in retirement, I suspect I will also find many non-health care related technological inefficiencies and blunders that I may be able to effect with programming changes.
